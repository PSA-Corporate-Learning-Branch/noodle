The colonial political system

In Canada, the colonial political system includes a governing party, a seat of government, jurisdictions (areas governed), laws and residents. Crown government is responsible for making and implementing laws, managing public affairs, providing public services, and representing the interests of Canadian residents.

Crown government

Crown government refers to government established through colonization, in what is now known as Canada. It includes the British government and monarchy (the Crown), the Government of Canada, territories and provincial governments.

This course outlines the Crown government systems, of which we are a part.

What is a constitutional monarchy?

Canada's system of government is a constitutional monarchy (opens a new window). It's a parliamentary system of government based on the Westminster tradition (opens a new window). The Canadian monarch is the same person as the British monarch, King Charles III. He is the King of Canada and the constitutional head of state.

Governor General

The Governor General represents the King in Canada at the federal level. The Lieutenant Governors are the King's representatives at the provincial level. They are appointed by the Governor General through an Order-in-Council. The Prime Minister proposes the Order-in-Council and the federal cabinet approves.

There are three layers to the Crown Government system in Canada: federal (Government of Canada), provincial (B.C. provincial government) and local or municipal government.

Each layer has its own set of roles and responsibilities. They work together to serve the needs of the residents but operate independently within its jurisdiction.

Federal

The Government of Canada (opens a new window):

  • Provides national social services
  • Supports the economy
  • Maintains national defence and security
  • Establishes criminal law

Federal government also works to renew the nation-to-nation, Inuit-Crown, and government-to-government relationships between Canada and First Nations, Inuit and Métis. It is responsible for modernizing Government of Canada structures to enable Indigenous Peoples to build capacity and to support their vision of self-determination.

Provincial

The B.C. provincial government (opens a new window):

  • Establishes provincial laws
  • Supports B.C.'s economy
  • Provides public services to support residents, including health care
  • Manages natural resources
  • Builds working relationships with Indigenous Peoples and other governments

Local

Local and municipal governments (opens a new window), including cities and regional districts:

  • Provide community planning
  • Create and enforce bylaws
  • Provide essential local services such as clean water, sewer systems, parks and recreation, and fire protection

Branches of the provincial government

The B.C. provincial government has three branches: legislative, executive, and judicial. Together these branches govern and manage the province.

Legislative

The legislative branch enacts laws, gives authority to cabinet, and examines the government's actions. The Lieutenant Governor, Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs), and the Head of the Opposition work within the legislative branch.

Executive

The executive branch is made up of B.C. Executive Council (known as cabinet) and the BC Public Service. The Premier (head of the provincial government) is both the first minister and the chief officer of the executive branch.

Judicial

The judicial branch is independent of the executive and legislative branches of provincial government. Judges and Associate Judges work within the judicial system.

The Legislative branch

The Legislative Assembly is B.C.'s provincial parliament. Currently there are 93 elected MLAs in the assembly. As a group, they have the power to debate and pass laws.

Operational roles

The Speaker and Officers of the Legislative Assembly perform important operational roles. For example, the Legislative Assembly adopts rules of parliamentary procedure called Standing Orders. The orders are designed to ensure all MLAs can take part in orderly Chamber debates.

Legislative Assembly etiquette

The Speaker enforces the standing orders which require MLAs to show respect for the Speaker and for each other. For example, members must not speak unless "recognized" (allowed to speak) by the Speaker. Members must not interrupt when the Speaker is speaking.

The Executive branch

The Executive Branch is generally referred to as the provincial government. The executive branch is made up of the Lieutenant Governor, the Premier, Cabinet, and the BC Public Service. The Premier acts as the head of the provincial government. The Premier is both the first minister and the chief officer of the executive branch.

Cabinet

The B.C. Executive Council, also known as Cabinet, consists of the Premier and all the Ministers. Led by the Premier, together the Premier and Ministers run the provincial government. Cabinet sets priorities and works similarly to a board of directors. The Premier recommends MLAs (typically from their party), and the Lieutenant Governor appoints them as Cabinet ministers. Each Minister is responsible for a specific service area or ministry.

Ministers

All ministers take an oath of allegiance and confidentiality. They declare that they will not reveal confidential cabinet discussions when assuming office. Maintaining cabinet confidence extends to any public servant who attends cabinet or cabinet committee meetings or contributes to cabinet submissions. The minister is responsible to the Legislative Assembly for the actions and performance of their organization.

Individual ministerial responsibility

Ministers exercise considerable decision-making power. They are constitutionally responsible for the provision and conduct of provincial government. This is called the principle of individual ministerial responsibility. This principle ensures ministers are held accountable to the legislature and the public. The Legislative Assembly holds ministers personally and collectively responsible. Ministers are accountable for their own actions and for the actions of those within their ministries.

The role of cabinet

Cabinet is established under section 9 of the Constitution Act. It deliberates and makes decisions on proposals specific to government policy and legislative priorities, appointments, and overall provincial government direction.

The Judicial branch

The judicial system is independent of the executive and legislative branches of provincial government.

Governments must follow the law

In the Canadian political system, Crown governments are not above the law.

Courts do not “make” policy, but they do have power to override laws and decisions that violate the Constitution including precedents established by past decisions or violating due process.

Accountability

Courts and the judicial system have authority to interpret laws, hear cases to determine innocence, guilt or liability. They hold the provincial government accountable.

Provincial government policy teams consult with legal counsel to ensure any newly proposed policy and legislation follow the law.

Indigenous Peoples

Within what is now known as Canada, Indigenous Peoples include First Nations, Inuit, and Métis.

First Nations and Inuit have lived here since time immemorial, long before Crown government was established. First Nations and Inuit are distinctive societies that pre-existed the arrival of Europeans as sovereigns over their territories.

Métis emerged as a distinctive people with their own customs and ways of life. They are recognized as separate from their First Nations or Inuit and European ancestors.

Indigenous Peoples have the right to self-determination and self-government, as affirmed in the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples and the B.C. Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act.

Diverse expression of rights

First Nations, Métis, and Inuit within Canada express these rights in diverse ways. This means they have diverse governance structures, laws and legal orders, cultures, languages, histories, customs, and practices. They manage the lands and resources in their territories. They see to the needs of their members and citizens and they make treaties and alliances with their neighbours.

Colonialism in Canada has suppressed these rights. Crown government structures have imposed governance systems onto Indigenous Peoples in Canada. The Crown has asserted sovereignty over Indigenous Peoples and territories.

Implementing the UN Declaration

As part of its work to create true and lasting reconciliation with Indigenous Peoples in B.C., the provincial government is implementing the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UN Declaration) and adopting the Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada's Calls to Action.

The B.C. provincial government is working to address the consequences of colonial policies which have had lasting effects on all Indigenous Peoples: First Nations, Métis and Inuit.

The Declaration Act

The Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act (Declaration Act) establishes the UN Declaration as the Province's framework for reconciliation, as called for by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission's Calls to Action. Through this Act, the province has adopted a distinctions-based approach to advancing reconciliation and implementing the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples.

A distinction-based approach means that the Province's work with First Nations, Métis, and Inuit will be conducted in a manner that acknowledges the specific rights, interests, priorities and concerns of each, while respecting and acknowledging these distinct Peoples with unique cultures, histories, rights, laws, and governments.

A new relationship

Reconciliation requires a government-to-government relationship between the B.C. government and Indigenous governing bodies.

The role of BC Public Service employees

As BCPS employees, we are representatives of the province of British Columbia.

All ministries and public service professionals are expected to be capable and competent to partner with Indigenous Peoples and work toward lasting and meaningful reconciliation.

B.C.'s general elections

Under the Constitution Act, B.C. holds a general election every four years. The elected governing party may choose to hold one sooner or the Legislative Assembly can call an election sooner if it loses confidence in the current government.

A general election officially begins when the writs of election are issued for each of the province's electoral districts.

Interregnum

The issuing of the writs also signals the beginning of the interregnum period. The interregnum period starts when the Legislative Assembly is dissolved until the incoming government is established. It has been described as a 'caretaker' period for government. During this time, all major actions and decisions are deferred and only routine ministry business continues. Programs and services remain operational for the residents of B.C.

Learn more: Your role during interregnum

As a BCPS employee, you must ensure you maintain the Standards of Conduct and fulfil your responsibilities in a non-partisan manner. Learn more about Elections: Roles and process on Compass.

Electoral districts

The province is divided into electoral districts. During an election, residents vote for someone in their district who they feel will best represent their interests. The person who wins the most votes in a district becomes a member of the Legislative Assembly, or an MLA.

Elections BC

Elections BC administers provincial general elections, by-elections, recall petitions, initiative petitions, initiative votes, and referenda votes. Elections BC also oversees campaign financing and advertising rules at the local level. It is an independent and non-partisan Office of the Legislature.

The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms gives Canadian citizens the right to vote in provincial and federal elections. Elections BC ensures that right for the residents of B.C.

Women won the right to vote in some provincial elections in 1916 and in 1918, most Caucasian women had the right to vote in federal elections. It was not until 1960 that all Canadians were granted the right to vote including Indigenous men and women.

After the election

After voting and counting are completed, the Chief Electoral Officer submits a list of the elected candidates to the Clerk of the Legislative Assembly. The Clerk then oversees the ceremony for the swearing-in of the new Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs).

Once the Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) are sworn in, provincial government can form. Provincial government is made up of the governing party, the official opposition and the MLAs.

The governing party

The Lieutenant Governor invites the leader of the political party that has the confidence of the Legislative Assembly to form provincial government. Usually, that is the party that has elected the most MLAs. The leader of that party becomes the Premier.

The Official Opposition

The party with the second highest number of MLAs (opposition party with most seats) is known as His Majesty's Loyal Opposition (or the Official Opposition). Even though they have different ideas on how government should be run, they remain loyal to the Crown.

The opposition (along with MLAs from other parties) hold the governing party accountable by questioning decisions and presenting alternatives.

Members of the Legislative Assembly

Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs) are elected across the province.

An MLA's job is to represent the constituency that elected them. When the Legislative Assembly is in session, MLAs collaborate to govern the province. They research, debate, and vote on all proposed laws (bills) and budgets. MLAs consider the views and concerns of their district and of all B.C. residents.

In addition to representing their constituents, MLAs work to fulfil the primary functions of the Legislative Assembly.

Functions of the Legislative Assembly

Making laws

Any MLA may propose a new law (called a bill). Most bills are introduced by cabinet ministers and are called public bills.

MLAs are responsible for studying and debating all bills put before them. If the majority of MLAs support a bill, it will eventually become an Act of the Legislature, otherwise known as a law.

Royal Assent

A bill does not become law until the Lieutenant Governor grants Royal Assent (the King's official approval) to a bill passed by the Legislative Assembly.

An Act can come into force immediately after Royal Assent, or at a later date as set in a commencement section of the Act. This gives the people affected by the law more time to prepare for the change.

Approving finances

Cabinet informs the Legislative Assembly of the financial requirements of the government. In turn, the Legislative Assembly authorizes the supply (grants of money) and the taxes. No tax may be imposed or money spent without the consent of the Legislative Assembly.

Estimates

The Lieutenant Governor requests money, known as "Estimates" on behalf of ministries program funding and payroll. The Minister of Finance presents the requests to the Assembly.

MLAs are responsible for debating and voting on the Estimates as part of this budget process.

Scrutinizing the governing party

Every Monday to Thursday when the Legislative Assembly is in session, MLAs participate in Question Period.

Question Period

This is an intense thirty-minute period of rapid-fire questions and answers. It is time dedicated for the opposition to question the Premier and cabinet ministers about provincial government plans and activities.

Parliamentary committee work

There are two types of parliamentary committees: select standing committees and special committees.

Small groups of MLAs from all parties are appointed to form committees. They review particular issues in more detail than is possible in the larger Assembly.

Usually, these committees do not include either the Premier or cabinet ministers. Most MLAs sit on at least one committee.

In the community

When the Legislative Assembly is not in session, MLAs will often return to their communities. They learn about issues and concerns from their constituents and work on their behalf.

Cabinet Ministers

Cabinet Ministers are the MLAs in charge of running provincial government ministries. The Premier is responsible for recommending cabinet members for appointment based on their abilities, expertise, and other considerations.

Supply Bills

Interim Supply Bill

An “interim supply” bill is passed so that ministries have spending authority at the start of the fiscal year (April 1). This ensures continuity of services and programs while the Estimates debate is ongoing.

Final Supply Bill

Once the debate is finished, a vote is held to determine if that ministry's budget is approved. A final Supply Bill is then introduced in the House. The approved expenditures become the ministry's budget for the fiscal year.

Fiscal Accountability and Transparency

The speech from the throne, the Budget Day speech and estimates are all publicly broadcast on the Legislature website. Transcripts of the speeches are available on the website as well.

Treasury Board

The Treasury Board is chaired by the Minister of Finance. The board handles financial decisions on behalf of the Executive Council. The Treasury Board also ensures fiscal accountability throughout provincial government. This is a legislated committee under the Financial Administration Act.

Financial Administration Act

The Act gives the Treasury Board broad fiscal management powers. This includes expenditures, revenues, debt management, capital investments and asset management, accounting policies and practices, as well as the economy, efficiency and effectiveness of provincial government programs. More broadly, it provides oversight on the effective management and allocation of public funds to support key provincial government priorities and programs.

Taxes

In addition to chairing the Treasury Board, the Minister of Finance also has sole responsibility for taxation policy in the province. Taxes help to fund the government programs and services that residents of B.C. rely on. They support the economic and social objectives of government and as a result, influence the financial decisions of residents and businesses.

Learn more: Finance Foundations

Financial competency is every public service employee's responsibility. Even if you're not working in finance, every decision you make has a financial impact. Whether you are developing policies, implementing programs, or working at a service desk, your choices impact the management of public money.

Learn more about finances in the BC Public Service and your role in the Finance Foundations course on the LearningHUB.

Decision-making in the B.C. provincial government follows a structured and informed approach. Making decisions in the form of legislation, regulations, directives, policies, and strategies are core provincial government functions.

Strategic direction

Here's an overview of how decisions are made:

  • The governing party provides strategic direction to the provincial government through ministers’ mandate letters, the throne speech and government’s strategic plan
  • Cabinet provides direction on fulfilling provincial government priorities and commitments
  • Cabinet also makes regulations, appoints officials and provides overall provincial government direction

Cabinet's role

Major policy decisions and all proposals for legislation come to cabinet for discussion and decision. Much of cabinet's business is handled through cabinet committees. Cabinet could not operate effectively if all proposals were brought to the full cabinet table.

Cabinet committees discuss and approve proposals specific to provincial government priorities. Cabinet committee decisions are reported to cabinet as recommendations. These recommendations are not final until approved (ratified) by cabinet.

Policy and legislation initiatives go through a detailed decision-making process for cabinet approval.

Treasury Board's role

The Treasury Board handles financial decisions on behalf of the Executive Council. They consider cabinet's direction and competing government priorities to approve:

  • Scope, scale and approach
  • Implementation and timelines
  • Resources (funding and positions)
  • Performance management expectations on outcomes

Learn more: Decision-making process

Review the Cabinet Process Map to see the sequence of steps, approvals, and milestones that are part of the decision-making process.

Mandate letters

When a minister is appointed, they receive a mandate letter issued by the Premier. A mandate letter outlines the minister's priorities and objectives for the term. The letter states shared priorities such as working toward true and meaningful reconciliation with Indigenous Peoples. The content of Mandate letters may change depending on the government of the day.

Service plans

Each ministry then creates a three-year service plan. The plan outlines the strategic priorities, goals and deliverables based on the mandate.

Learn more: Mandate letters

Locate and review your ministry's mandate letter. Consider the priorities and objectives laid out in the letter and how they connect to your work.

Ensuring accountability is a priority for the BC Public Service. Independent, non-partisan organizations provide oversight, conduct investigations, and perform audits for provincial government programs and services. The Ombudsperson, Auditor General, and the Information and Privacy Commissioner for B.C. ensure access and privacy laws are followed, building trust and accountability in BC Public Service.

Ombudsperson

The Ombudsperson is an independent officer of the legislature. They are tasked with investigating complaints from members of the public. Complaints can pertain to the administration of provincial government and its programs and services.

Under the Public Interest Disclosure Act (PIDA), the Ombudsperson can also investigate allegations of wrongdoing and reprisal brought forward by current and former BCPS employees.

Auditor General

The Auditor General is a non-partisan and independent officer of the legislature. Appointed by elected members (MLAs), they serve for a single term of eight years.

The office of the Auditor General performs financial and performance audits for more than 160 organizations that make up provincial government. They include ministries, Crown corporations, universities, colleges, school districts and health authorities. The audits reveal how well provincial government is managing its responsibilities and resources.

Information and Privacy Commissioner for B.C.

The Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner (OIPC) provides independent oversight and enforcement of B.C.'s access and privacy laws: the Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act (FOIPPA) and the Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA).

Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act (FOIPPA)

The Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act (FOIPPA) regulates the information and privacy practices of "public bodies" including provincial government ministries, local governments, Crown corporations, local police forces, schools, and hospitals.

FOIPPA requires public bodies to have privacy management programs. They must report affected individual’s privacy breaches to the Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner (OIPC).

Office of the Merit Commissioner

The Merit Commissioner is an independent office that ensures fair and merit-based appointments in the BC Public Service.

They audit appointments and review staffing decisions when requested by unsuccessful applicants. They also check that dismissal processes follow the required rules and procedures. These two main lines of oversight are outlined in the Public Service Act.

A responsibility for reconciliation

Reconciliation is a collective priority for the roles and responsibilities within the provincial government and the BC Public Service. Cabinet, Ministers, and Cabinet Committees are responsible for demonstrating and upholding accountability to the Legislative Assembly's Reconciliation Action Plan. This responsibility is fundamental and integrated throughout all work as a core value.

Reconciliation Action Plan

The Legislative Assembly released its first Reconciliation Action Plan on May 8, 2024. It outlines the Legislative Assembly's commitments and actions towards reconciliation with Indigenous Peoples. It recognizes the need to address historical injustices by acknowledging the past. It demonstrates the desire to build new relationships with Indigenous Peoples based on respect and understanding.

Through reconciliation initiatives, the Legislative Assembly is working to promote and include Indigenous cultures, languages, traditions, and representation within its institution and its proceedings.

A Closer Look

Cabinet

  • Central decision-making body for executive branch
  • Provides direction for policy, budget, strategic priorities, and substantive issues

Ministers

  • Implement government direction through legislation, policy and programs
  • Spend on behalf of government, based on voted appropriations and government direction
  • Get approval from cabinet on proposed new legislation, policy or program decisions
  • Get approval from Treasury Board for any changes to the Ministry's fiscal plan

Cabinet Committees

  • Established by the Premier to support effective operations and decision-making by cabinet. Two cabinet committees, the  Treasury Board and the Environment and Land Use Committee, are required by statute. Additional cabinet committees may be established to meet general or specific needs at the Premier’s prerogative
  • Parliamentary committees are part of the Legislative branch and appointed by the Legislative Assembly
  • Consider proposals aimed at implementing provincial government priorities
  • Make recommendations to the cabinet for approval
  • Ensure a diversity of Indigenous perspectives are represented as appropriate, using distinctions-based approach.

Speech from the Throne

B.C. Legislative Assembly formally opens the new season with the Lieutenant Governor's reading of the throne speech.

The throne speech is written by the government of the day to explain why the Legislative Assembly is being assembled. It outlines the government's broad goals for the coming legislative session. This occurs in February of each year.

Budget

The Minister of Finance presents the budget, typically, by the fourth Tuesday in February and after the throne speech. This is known as “Budget Day". It's when the provincial government shares how it will allocate its resources for the next three years. The Minister of Finance will announce any changes in taxation policy.

Budget Day includes a “lock-up” where reporters and select interested parties receive a preview of the budget. Questions about the budget can be asked of government staff and the minister. All content remains confidential until the minister tables the budget in the legislature later that day.

Vote in favour

The contents of the budget are then debated in the Legislative Assembly for a maximum of six days. Once the budget debate concludes, a vote on the motion is held.

A vote in support of the motion is a vote in favour of the provincial government's financial policies and is considered a vote of confidence in the government of the day.

Estimates

Once the Legislative Assembly votes in favour of the motion, the Estimates Debate process can begin. Estimates debates take place in the "Committee of Supply." Here the budget is debated ministry by ministry. Ministers attend the legislature and respond to questions and challenges from the opposition.

Estimate Notes

Each ministry's effort to prepare a minister for estimates is substantial. The work extends across all ministries to develop comprehensive Estimates notes. These include specific information about programs, services, issues and impacts. Ministries must anticipate the opposition party's questions, to prepare and support the minister.

The effort to build and administer the budget is significant. It involves many employees across all ministries. Depending on your role, you may be one of the many BCPS employees involved in this effort.

The BC Public Service

The public service is a professional organization that supports and delivers the priorities of the elected provincial government. It is politically neutral and remains in place through elections. The public service is responsible to the elected provincial government.

The public service is traditionally “anonymous.” Ministers answer to provincial parliament and to the public for government actions, usually without naming the public servants who provided the advice or who carried out the administrative action.

The BCPS is part of the executive branch of provincial government. Core government ministries, agencies, boards and commissions all make up the executive branch. Public service employees serve the public's interest in a way that maintains and enhances trust. We provide a reliable presence for B.C. residents during times of change, continuously adapting our work to reflect evolving provincial government priorities.

Political leaders elected by the public provide strategic direction. Public service employees turn their direction into action by:

  • Developing and delivering policies and programs
  • Carrying out provincial government decisions and policies
  • Upholding the rights of Indigenous Peoples in B.C.
  • Providing government with honest and objective advice
  • Responsibly handling of public resources and information

Ministries

The BCPS is divided into ministries. Each ministry is responsible for a specific area of public policy, government function and/or service delivery.

Learn more: Ministries

The number, size and composition of ministries change over time, as the priorities of government change. Check out the full list of current ministries.

Executive leadership structure

Unlike most other public servants, executive leaders in the public service are usually order-in-council (OIC) appointments under the provisions of the Public Service Act. In the public service sector, executive positions are collectively known as "corporate executive."

An OIC is a government order recommended by the Executive Council and signed by the Lieutenant Governor. While they are appointed, executives are professional and non-partisan public servants bound by the same Oath and Standards of Conduct as all public service employees.

The Deputy Minister to the Premier (DMP)

Also known as: Cabinet Secretary, Head of the Public Service
Reports to: Premier of B.C.

Responsibilities
  • Most senior unelected (non-partisan) official in the public service
  • Serves as a connection between government and the public service, as well as fostering government-to-government relations
  • Oversees BCPS employees
  • Offers advice to the Premier and the cabinet on how to put their agenda into action

Deputy Ministers (DMs)

Reports to: Head of the Public Service
Accountable to: Their Minister

Responsibilities
  • Serves the provincial government of the day and their minister
  • Non-partisan senior public servants
  • Acts on cabinet decisions and advises the minister
  • Oversees ministry operations
  • Role comparable to that of a Chief Executive Officer (CEO) in a private organization

Associate Deputy Ministers

Reports to: Deputy Minister or the Deputy Minister to the Premier

Associate Deputy Ministers that report directly to the DMP do not have a Deputy Minister in the ministry. They lead ministries that are typically smaller in size and are members of the Deputy Ministers' Council (DMC). Associate Deputy Ministers that report directly to a Deputy Minister usually work in a large ministry.

Responsibilities
  • Accountable for meeting ministry deliverables, managing resources within a budget, and may also have statutory responsibilities, as outlined in legislation
  • Provides advice and support to the Deputy Minister to the Premier (DMP) and/or Deputy Ministers
  • Recommends and implements government policies, plans strategies, and provides leadership for the organization

Assistant Deputy Ministers (ADMs)

Reports to: Deputy Minister

Responsibilities
  • Works closely with the Deputy Minister to manage the day-to-day operations of a division within their ministry
  • Ensures that work is aligned with the provincial government's strategic direction
  • Offers advice to leadership and can step in for the DM when necessary
  • Role comparable to that of a vice president in a private organization

Executive Leads (EL)

Reports to: Assistant Deputy Minister and Deputy Minister

Responsibilities
  • Created where successful project or initiative implementation is key to ensuring public trust and confidence is maintained and enhanced
  • Action ministry-specific or cross-ministry business goals while ensuring alignment with government priorities
  • Provide key contributions and commit to action the business goals of government
  • Time-limited role established to lead and support the delivery of key projects or initiatives

The role provides an ideal experiential learning opportunity for those considering or being considered for future executive roles in the BC Public Service.

Public service careers offer a unique opportunity to contribute to meaningful work that directly impacts the lives of B.C. residents. These roles are defined by a strong sense of purpose, collaboration, and the chance to shape the future of the province.

Public service employees are required to uphold the highest professional and ethical standards, values and obligations regardless of their personal and political views.

Oath of Employment

Each BCPS employee takes an Oath of Employment when they first join the public service. When taking the Oath, they are reminded of the duties, powers and trusts that are placed in public servants. It also highlights the importance of ethics, impartiality and integrity in all that we do as members of the BCPS.

As part of the Oath, public servants swear to abide by the Standards of Conduct and:

  • Serve ethically and with integrity, loyalty, impartiality, and objectivity.
  • Put the interests of the public and the public service ahead of their own personal interests.
  • Maintain and enhance the public's trust and confidence in the public service. In addition to the Oath of Employment, public servants are expected to follow the law including the Public Service Act and Standards of Conduct for BCPS employees.

Standards of Conduct

BCPS employees must demonstrate the highest standards of conduct. Their conduct must instill confidence and trust and not bring BCPS into disrepute. The honesty and integrity of the BCPS demands the impartiality of employees in the conduct of their duties. It is a condition of employment to comply with these standards of conduct.

All BCPS employees should be familiar with the Standards of Conduct in full and what they mean for both their professional and personal lives.

The Standards of Conduct covers areas such as:

  • Loyalty
  • Confidentiality
  • Public comments
  • Political activity
  • Service to the public
  • Workplace behaviour
  • Conflicts of interest

Public Service Ethics

BCPS employees are united by a shared commitment to deliver the services, programs and policies of the provincial government in ways that maintain the trust and confidence of the people of British Columbia. Public servants work in a variety of different settings across the province. We are all bound by an ethical framework that is woven into the fabric of our organization. The BC Public Service must model the highest ethical standards as our society evolves and faces new challenges.

Where we all belong

Organizational culture is the set of values, beliefs, attitudes, systems, and rules that outline and influence employee behaviour within an organization. It is often described as “how things work around here”.

In the BCPS, our commitment to reconciliation, equity, diversity, and inclusion (REDI) are an integral part of our organizational culture. We are committed to building and supporting a workforce reflective of our province, inclusive of Indigenous Peoples, minority communities, immigrants, persons with disabilities and the 2SLGBTQ+ community.

Building a public service where we all belong results in both a healthy and vibrant work culture and better outcomes for people living in British Columbia as well.

The strategy

The Where We All Belong strategy sets the corporate direction on equity, diversity, and inclusion as part of the BCPS's genuine commitment to transformational changes in our organizational culture.

In addition to this corporate strategy, every ministry has equity, diversity and inclusion goals and action plans to implement within their organization.

BC Public Service employees at all levels (employees, supervisors, leaders and executives) have a pivotal role to play in supporting the development of our inclusive organization. Whether you are brand new to the BCPS or a seasoned employee, know your key responsibilities to support this commitment and create a work environment where everyone belongs.

Shared values

Public service employees work in a wide variety of jobs across different ministries, united by a set of shared values. Out of the seven public service values, Integrity has been defined as our overarching value. Integrity is the foundation for trust. The residents of British Columbia place a high degree of trust in public service employees to provide essential services, steward natural resources, and manage sensitive and personal information with care.

Public servants must do their best to act with integrity in both their personal and professional lives to ensure established and continued trust in the public service. Establishing trust within the public service fosters open, honest communication and supports an environment where it is safe to take risks and face challenges together.

There are six additional corporate values (video) that shape the public service culture: Curiosity, Service, Passion, Teamwork, Accountability, and Courage.

The corporate plan sets the strategic vision and guiding direction for the BC Public Service as it continues to evolve. You can find the corporate values and more in the 2023 Corporate HR Plan, Where Ideas Work.

Glossary

Glossary index: A B C D E G J L M O P Q R S T W

A

Act
Also called a statute. A bill that has been approved by the Legislative Assembly.
Amendment
A proposal to alter a motion, bill, or committee report that must be adopted by the House before the proposed changes take effect.
Assistant Deputy Ministers (ADMs)
ADMs report to the Deputy Minister, manage day-to-day ministry or division operations to ensure work is aligned with the provincial governments' strategic direction.
Associate Deputy Minister
Associate Deputy Ministers provide advice and support to the Deputy Minister to the Premier (DMP) and/or Deputy Minister. They recommend and implement government policies, plan strategies, and provide leadership for the organization.
Auditor General
A non-partisan, independent officer of the legislature responsible for performing financial and performance audits for more than 160 organizations that make up provincial government.

B

B.C. Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act
The Declaration Act mandates government to bring provincial laws into alignment with the UN Declaration and to develop and implement an action plan to achieve the objectives of the UN Declaration in consultation and co-operation with Indigenous Peoples.
BC Public Service (BCPS)
The public service is a professional organization that supports the elected government. It is politically neutral and remains in place through elections.
Bill
Proposed legislation that is presented to the Legislative Assembly for its consideration and approval.
Budget
The government’s plan for fiscal, economic and social policies for the forthcoming fiscal year presented by the Minister of Finance in the Legislative Assembly.
Budget Debate
The general debate on the government’s fiscal, economic and social policies that follows the Minister of Finance’s budget address/speech.

C

Cabinet
Members of the governing party who have been appointed ministers (heads of government ministries). It is also called the Executive Council.
Cabinet Minister
A member of the governing party, appointed to the Executive Council by the Lieutenant Governor on the advice of the Premier.
Cabinet Process Map
A map illustrating the sequence of approvals for project for initiatives.
Constitutional monarchy
A parliamentary system of government based on the Westminster tradition.
Crown Corporation
B.C.’s Crown corporations are public-sector organizations established and funded by the Government of British Columbia to provide specialized goods and services to citizens. These include the Bank of Canada, the Canada Council for the Arts, the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation, Telefilm Canada, the International Development Research Centre, and the National Arts Centre Corporation.
Crown government
Refers to government established within what is now known as Canada, through colonization. It includes the British government and monarchy (the Crown), the Government of Canada and the Province of British Columbia.
Crown
His Majesty the King, represented by the Lieutenant Governor the in the executive branch of government.

D

Deputy Minister to the Premier
Also known as the Cabinet Secretary and Head of the Public Service. The Deputy Minister to the Premiers role falls within the Office of the Premier. This senior unelected official in the public service, and they serve as a connection between government and the public service.
Deputy Ministers (DMs)
Similar to a Chief Executive Officer, DMs are non-partisan senior public servants who report to the Head of the Public Service. They are accountable to their ministers, oversee ministry operations, act on cabinet decisions and advise the minister.

E

Estimates
The expenditure plans of all government ministries, consisting of Main Estimates tabled annually and Supplementary Estimates tabled as required. The print version of the Estimates is commonly referred to as the “blue book”.
Executive Branch
The political executive is composed of the Premier and Cabinet, who together run the government.
Executive Council
B.C. Executive Council, also called the Cabinet of British Columbia, includes the Lieutenant Governor in Council, the Premier, and Cabinet Ministers.

G

Governing Party
The political party that wins the largest number of seats in an election. Its leader is called upon by the Lieutenant Governor to serve as Premier and form a government.

J

Judicial Branch
The judicial system is independent of the executive and legislative branches of provincial government.

L

Legislative Assembly
The lawmaking body of British Columbia, consisting of all the elected Members, as constituted under the provincial Constitution Act. Also known as the Legislative Branch.
Legislative Assembly Management Committee (LAMC)
The governing body of the Legislative Assembly of British Columbia. Chaired by the Speaker, the Committee derives its powers from the Legislative Assembly Management Committee Act and is responsible for the Legislative Assembly’s financial and administrative policies.
Legislature
Formally, the Lieutenant Governor acting by and with the advice and consent of the Legislative Assembly. The term is also commonly used to refer to the provincial parliament buildings which house the chamber where debates take place, the legislative library, and offices of staff and MLAs.
Lieutenant Governor
Lieutenant Governors are the King's representatives in each province. They are appointed by the Governor General through an Order-in-Council that is proposed by the Prime Minister and approved by the federal Cabinet. The Lieutenant Governor’s approval (Royal Assent) is needed for a Bill to become law.

M

Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs)
A Member of the Legislative Assembly who is elected to represent, in the provincial parliament, the citizens of his or her riding.
Ministers
Implement government direction through legislation, policy and programs. Ministers are authorized to spend on behalf of government based on voted appropriations and government direction.

O

Official Opposition
The party with the second highest number of MLAs (opposition party with most seats) is known as His Majesty's Loyal Opposition (or the Official Opposition).
Order in Council
An order issued by the Lieutenant Governor in Council (Cabinet) regarding the administration of government or appointments to office and other matters.

P

Parliament
The period between general elections composed of sessions that are made up of individual sittings in the House; for example, The 36th Parliament, 2nd Session. Also, a term commonly used to refer to the Legislative Assembly.
Parliamentary Committee
An all-party group of Private Members appointed by the House to investigate and report on matters referred by the Legislative Assembly.
Premier
The first minister and chief officer of the executive branch. The Premier acts as the head of government for B.C.
Proclamation
An official notice or order issued by the Lieutenant Governor. Parliament starts and ends by proclamation.

Q

Question Period
A 30-minute proceeding held every Monday to Thursday afternoon while the House is in session. Members of the Legislative Assembly question cabinet ministers about government priorities and policies.

R

Regulation
Whereas an Act provides the policy framework, the regulation(s) under the Act provide the details to give effect to the policy. Not all Acts have regulations. Sometimes regulations are used to bring Acts into force.
Resolution
A motion adopted by the House to make a declaration of opinion or purpose.
Royal Assent
The ceremony in which the Lieutenant Governor formally approves a bill passed by the House. To have legal effect, all bills must be given Royal Assent after they have passed the third reading. The Lieutenant Governor assents to bills on behalf of the King.

S

Service Plan
The plan outlines the strategic priorities, goals and deliverables based on the mandate.
Speech from the Throne
A speech delivered by the Lieutenant Governor at the start of a session. It provides a broad outline of the government’s plans and priorities.
Standards of Conduct
A condition of employment for all BC Public service employees. The standards require BCPS employees to demonstrate the highest standards of conduct and act impartiality of employees in the conduct of their duties.
Standing Orders
Permanent written rules adopted by the Legislative Assembly to govern its proceedings and the proceedings of Committees. They may only be altered or repealed by a decision of the House.

T

Treasury Board
The board staff are responsible for developing and reviewing government's economic and fiscal policies and provides analysis and advice to the Treasury Board and to the Minister of Finance.

W

Westminster Model or Westminster Tradition
A system of parliamentary government originating in Britain, characterized by a symbolic head of state (monarch) and a political executive drawn from and directly responsible to the elected legislative body.
Writ
A writ of election is an official order from the Chief Electoral Officer and the Lieutenant Governor telling a District Electoral Officer to run an election. This order signals the start of an election. The election period ends when all the writs are returned.
